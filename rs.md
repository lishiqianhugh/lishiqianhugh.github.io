# Research Statement

Newton needed 20 years to formalize the laws of motion; a toddler, having never heard the word "gravity," already knows that a ball thrown upward will come back down. This gap — between the formal complexity of physics and the effortless ease of human physical intuition — hints at something profound: the human mind runs an internal **"world model"**, a mental engine that encodes not visual patterns but the **causal, physical structure of reality**. My research seeks to reverse-engineer this capacity and build it into AI — creating systems that perceive, simulate, and act in the physical world with human-like generality.

## Understanding Physical Reasoning: Cognitive Foundations and Computational Benchmarks

Before building a physical world model, one must first ask: what would it even mean for a machine to "understand" physics? I approached this question from both sides of the bridge between cognitive science and AI. On the computational side, I probed the learning mechanisms underlying physical reasoning (NeurIPS 2022 Spotlight) and uncovered a sobering finding: current deep learning systems, despite massive training, fail to acquire the compositional, generalizable physical knowledge that humans develop effortlessly. On the cognitive side, I proposed a dual-process model showing that human physical intuition is not a single monolithic ability but an interplay of fast heuristic judgment and deliberate mental simulation (CogSci 2025), and that humans adaptively allocate cognitive resources when planning physical actions (CogSci 2026). Bridging these insights, I built I-PHYRE (ICLR 2024), an interactive benchmark that demands not just predicting physics but intervening in it — shifting the standard from passive observation to active manipulation. Together, these works define the desiderata for a physically intelligent world model: it must generalize compositionally, reason at multiple levels of abstraction, and support goal-directed action.

## Physics-Grounded Dynamic Models

If the previous works define what a world model should do, the question becomes how — and specifically, what language should such a model think in? My answer: the language of **forces**. I developed Neural Force Field (NFF) (ICLR 2026), a framework that represents object interactions not as opaque latent vectors but as continuous, explicit force fields — the same mathematical objects that Newton and Euler used to describe the physical world, now learned end-to-end from data. With as few as a handful of examples, NFF abstracts gravity, support, and collision into composable force primitives that generalize to entirely unseen scenarios, enabling both forward simulation and backward planning through a single unified representation. I extended this idea from state space to pixel space with Neural Gaussian Force Fields (NGFF) (ICLR 2026), fusing 3D Gaussian perception with force-based dynamics to learn, simulate, and render interactive 4D worlds directly from video — orders of magnitude faster than classical simulators. In essence, NFF and NGFF give machines a "physics vocabulary" grounded in the same principles that govern reality.

## Physically Faithful Interactive World Models

These physics-grounded representations naturally lead to a more ambitious question: can we build generative models that not only faithfully simulate physical dynamics, but also allow users to **control and interact** with the generated world in real time? Current video generation models, despite producing visually stunning outputs, lack two capabilities essential for a true world model: fine-grained 3D controllability over how objects and cameras move, and the ability to respond to user input on the fly. My upcoming work tackles both challenges by unifying camera motion, object trajectories, and scene geometry into a single conditioning interface for video diffusion models, enabling joint camera-object control and real-time streaming generation — shifting the paradigm from "generate-then-watch" to truly interactive world simulation. Looking ahead, I envision this line of research converging with physics-grounded representations to produce **Interactive Physical World Models** — generative systems where users can specify how objects should move while the model itself infers physically plausible responses: pushing an object triggers realistic collisions, and novel interactions emerge from internalized physical laws rather than memorized patterns. Such models would serve as a unifying engine for embodied agents that plan by imagining physically faithful futures, robotic systems that simulate before acting, and creative tools that let users sculpt dynamic scenes interactively. 

Across this arc — from understanding physical cognition, to learning the language of forces, to building controllable generative worlds — a single thread runs through my work: **physics is not a constraint on imagination, but the very thing that makes imagination useful.** I aim to build world models that do not merely replay reality, but allow us to reach in and reshape it.
